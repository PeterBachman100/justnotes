Tiny Habits: The Small Changes that Change Everything

BJ Fogg

Synopsis

Building habits and creating positive change can be easy if you have the right approach. Don't rely on motivation and willpower. The only consistent, sustainable way to grow big is to start small and use incremental progress.

You change best by feeling good, not by feeling bad.

With Tiny Habits, you celebrate even the smallest successes. This takes advantage of our neurochemistry and quickly turns deliberate actions into automatic habits.

The essence of tiny habits is this: take a behavior you want, make it tiny, find where it fits naturally in your life, and nurture it's growth. Focus on small actions that you can do in less than thirty seconds. Feeling successful helps us quickly wire in new habits, lets them grow naturally, and motivates us to do more.

To be successful in changing your habits:

  1. Stop judging yourself
  2. Take you aspirations and break them down into tiny behaviors
  3. Celebrate even the smallest successes
  4. Embrace your mistakes as discoveries and use them to move forward

Fogg Behavior Model

A Behavior will happen if both Motivation and Ability are high enough when the Prompt occurs. When a behavior is prompted above the Action Line, it happens. Behaviors that ultimately become habits will reliably fall above the Action Line.

Behavior = Motivation + Ability + Prompt

When motivation is high, people can do very difficult things. When ability is high, people will do behaviors even with very low motivation. Without a prompt, something you are very motivated and capable of doing won't happen.

Motivation

Motivation is your desire to do the behavior. It can change based on many circumstances, and is often fickle. People often try to change it as a way to manipulate their behavior, but it is unreliable for creating habits.

Ability

Ability is your capacity to do the behavior when the prompt occurs. For example, I may be able to eat vegetables for dinner, but if I am prompted at 10am, the behavior won't occur at that moment. For another example, I may be prompted and motivated to lift 1000lbs, but I am not able to do that, so the behavior of lifting 1000lbs does not occur.

Prompt

Prompt is your cue to do the behavior. This could be an internal thing like just remembering, or another person telling you, and an alarm or email, or even a routine.

The ABC's of Tiny Habits

This formula may not look like much, but a well designed habit will take root quickly without much effort.

Anchor

The Anchor reminds you to do the Behavior. This is a term used for Prompts that are actions you reliably do in your life. More info below.

Behavior

You do the Behavior immediately after the Anchor Moment. It is important to tie these steps closely together in time. There shouldn't be a gap of more than a few seconds between the Anchor and the Behavior.

Celebration

You Celebrate immediately after doing the Behavior. This step makes a huge difference.

Celebrating your good behaviors may be the most important part of creating habits.

To effectively celebrate, it is critical that you feel an authentic sense of pride.

Behavior Design

Having systematic criteria for how to choose behaviors for yourself will make you effective in getting results, and the steps in Behavior Design will save you from guessing.

Behavior Design is a skill that you will build over time. Once you've practiced a couple times, you can expect to move through the bulk of the behavior design process in less than 20 minutes. Having a system in place helps you move from dreams to reality.

Steps in Behavior Design

1 / 7

Clarify The Aspiration

Motivation toward an abstraction doesn't yield results

Millions of people genuinely aspire to live healthier, live stressful, and more fulfilling lives. The problem is that people try to motivate themselves toward an aspiration. So people focus on aspirations and motivation, but that combo doesn't produce results. Investing time and energy to motivate ourselves toward an abstraction is the wrong move.

People don't naturally think in terms of specific behaviors. Aspirations are abstract desires, like wanting your kids to succeed in school. Outcomes are more measurable, like getting straight As second semester. Both of these are great places to start the process of Behavior Design. But aspirations and outcomes are not behaviors. Here’s an easy way to differentiate behaviors from aspirations and outcomes: A behavior is something you can do right now or at another specific point in time. You can turn off your phone. You can eat a carrot. You can open a textbook and read five pages. These are actions that you can do at any given moment. In contrast, you can’t achieve an aspiration or outcome at any given moment. You cannot suddenly get better sleep.

What do you want? What is your dream? What result do you want to achieve?

Getting clear on your aspiration allows you to design efficiently for what you really want. You might assume your aspiration is to be more mindful. But when you think about this, you decide that what you really want is to reduce stress in your life. And reducing stress will be easier than being more mindful. You could take a daily walk outside, play a musical instrument for ten minutes, or cut back on watching TV news. In this step, revise your aspiration or outcome so it taps into what really matters to you.

2 / 7

Explore Behavior Options

Come up with a bunch of specific behaviors that can help you achieve your aspiration. You are not making any decisions or commitments in this step. You are exploring your options. The more behaviors you list, the better. You can tap into your creativity or maybe ask friends for their ideas.

Magic Wanding

When guiding people in this process, I like to remind them that for now they have magical powers. They can get themselves to do any behavior.

It’s important to explore in this step—and be wildly optimistic.

I call this method Magic Wanding. Even with a magic wand in hand and my encouraging them to use those superpowers, people sometimes wish for practical behaviors (which is fine). Some wishes are behaviors you do one time: download a meditation app. Some wishes are for new habits: stretching for two minutes after every conference call. And some wishes are to stop doing a behavior: stop checking e-mail after seven p.m. To generate lots of behavior options, you can use the following categories during your own Magic Wanding sessions. What behaviors would you do one time? What new habits would you create? What habit would you stop? After you come up with each behavior wish, think to yourself, Great. What else? and keep going.

Look over your behavior wishes and try to make each one more specific. If you’ve written down “play with my dog” as a way to reduce stress, you could make that wish more specific by revising it to this: “Play fetch with my dog each evening at home.” After you revise your behavior wishes to be super specific (what I call “crispy”), move on to the next step in the Behavior Design process and get analytical and practical.

  1. Draw a cloud on a piece of paper.
  2. Write your inspiration inside a cloud.
  3. Come up with ten or more behaviors that would lead to your aspiration. Write each behavior outside the cloud, with an arrow pointing toward the cloud.
3 / 7

Match with Specific Behaviors

One big difference in Behavior Design versus other approaches is that with my methods you focus on habits you already have motivation to do. You don’t pick a habit and try to bolt on motivation later. In Behavior Design, motivation is already embedded in the new habit. In other approaches you will struggle to maintain a habit you think you should do. An important part of lasting change is matching yourself to behaviors you want to do, not just think you should do. We should be dreamy about our aspirations, but not about the behaviors that get us there.

The key question is: "Can I get myself to do this?"

In Behavior Design we have a name for the best matches: Golden Behaviors.

A Golden Behavior has three criteria:

  1. The behavior is effective in realizing your aspiration (impact)
  2. You want to do the behavior (motivation)
  3. You can do the behavior (ability)

In Behavior Design we match ourselves with new habits we can do even when we are at our most hurried, unmotivated, and beautifully imperfect. If you can imagine yourself doing the behavior on your hardest day of the week, it’s probably a good match. It’s probably a Golden Behavior.

Focus Mapping

  1. Write each behavior on a card of small piece of paper.
  2. Sort the behavior cards up and down along the impact dimension. Don't think about feasibility. Focus on the impact that the behaviors could have.
  3. Slide the behavior cards side to side along the feasibility dimension. Be realistic. Can you really get yourself to do these behaviors?
  4. Look in the upper right-hand corner. These are your Golden Behaviors.
4 / 7

Start Tiny

When you design a new habit, you are designing for consistency, for which simplicity is key. By going tiny, you create consistency, which makes sure the habit sticks.

When you are designing a new habit, you are really designing for consistency. And for that result, you’ll find that simplicity is the key. Or as I like to teach my students: Simplicity changes behavior. If you want to do a habit consistently, you’ve got to adjust the most reliable thing in the B=MAP model—ability. That’s where we have the most power to stack the deck in our favor. If a behavior is hard, make it easier to do. You’ll see that over time your motivation will vary, but your ability will improve the more you do your new habit. And that increase in ability helps your habit grow. Regardless of what your aspiration is, increasing your skills, getting tools and resources, and making the behavior tiny are what makes things easier to do.

How to make things Tiny

Starter Step

One small move toward the desired behavior. If you want to make a habit out of walking three miles every day, you starter Step might be putting your walking shoes on. Most days you'll head out the door and do some walking, but you don't have to. Do not raise the bar prematurely. Don't rush to make the behavior bigger. By keeping the bar low you keep the habit alive.

It's always okay to do the tiniest version of your habit. The tiny version is success.

Scaling Back

Take the behavior you want and shrink it. If your goal is to walk a mile, instead walk to the mailbox and back. Again, this is the habit. The tiny habit is success.

Don't raise the bar early, and don't beat yourself up for not doing extra. Growth will happen naturally.

Training

Learning more about the behavior can increase your ability. Examples are things like taking classes, reading books, and watching tutorials.

Tools & Resources

Make your environment work for you. If you struggle to floss, get a floss you like, and put it somewhere that you will see it. If you want to run every day, get comfortable shoes, workout clothes, rain gear, expert advice for a running plan, a song playlist you love, etc.

5 / 7

Find a Good Prompt

Prompts can be based off personal, internal cues (you feel hungry), external cues (a sticky note or alarm), or based off actions (your feet hit the floor after waking up). Action-based prompts are by far the most reliable. These are called Anchor Moments, or Anchors for short.

When using Anchors, try to find something that defines a precise, final moment of the action, called the Trailing Edge. For example, the Trailing Edge of brushing my teeth is putting my toothbrush back in the holder. Using the Trailing Edge of an Anchor is much more reliable than using the general moment.

For the best results, the Anchor (Prompt) and Behavior should have the same Location, Theme/Purpose, and Frequency.

The habit recipe: "After I sit down for work, I will change my car oil" will almost certainly fail because the location, frequency, and theme don't match.

Location

The anchor and behavior need to happen in the same physical location. The more precise the better. Doing both things at the same desk will work better than doing both in the same room, or same building.

Frequency

If you want to do something 4 times a day, match it with an anchor that happens 4 times a day.

Theme/Purpose

For the best results, match productivity behaviors with productivity anchors, relaxing behaviors with relaxing anchors, etc.

You can also use irritants and in-between times in your life as prompts for behaviors as well, such as hearing your coworker complain, or waiting in line. These are respectively called Pearl and Meanwhile habits.

6 / 7

Celebrate Successes

Feeling good is a vital part of the Tiny Habits method. You can create this good feeling by using a technique I call celebration. When you celebrate in the Tiny Habits way, you create a positive feeling inside yourself on demand. This good feeling wires the new habit into your brain. You’ll find that celebration is surprisingly effective, and it can be quick and easy, even fun.

Emotions Create Habits

When you celebrate effectively, you tap into the reward circuitry of your brain. By feeling good at the right moment, you cause your brain to recognize and encode the sequence of behaviors you just performed. In other words, you can hack your brain to create a habit by celebrating and self-reinforcing.

There is a direct connection between what you feel when you do a behavior and the likelihood that you will repeat the behavior in the future. Habits can form very quickly, often in just a few days, as long as people have a strong positive emotion connected to the behavior. In fact, some habits seem to get wired in immediately: You do the behavior once, and then you don’t consider other options again.

Conversely, if a behavior is painful, awkward, or unpleasant, your brain will rewire to start avoiding that habit. To keep a habit alive, celebrate extra when it is uncomfortable.

Search for celebrations that feel authentic to you. If you feel awkward or phony when celebrating, your attempts will backfire. Your brain doesn’t want to feel awkward—it wants to feel good. Celebrations are personal. What makes me feel good (and not lame) is probably different than what makes you feel good (and not lame).

It doesn’t matter whether your celebration is an explosive anthem or a silent focus on your life’s purpose. What matters is that your celebration creates the internal feeling of success.

For the sake of simplicity, I tell people to celebrate immediately after they do a behavior. But the truth is, you can build habits faster and more reliably by celebrating at three different times: the moment you remember to do the habit, when you’re doing the habit, and immediately after completing the habit.

Example Celebrations

  • Consider how your new habit helps you achieve your life's purpose.
  • Imagine getting the news that you got a new job.
  • Pause, breathe deeply, and appreciate your success.
  • Inhale and think of energy entering you.
  • Pound your chest.
  • Fist pump.
  • Thank God.
  • Play the song "Get Up" by T-pain.
  • Flex.
  • Say or think: "Good Job", "That's it", "YES", "F*** yeah"

Troubleshoot, Iterate, and Expand

Are you having trouble doing the habit?

The Ability Chain

Ask the question: "What is making the behavior hard to do?" One of these 5 items will be the limiting factor in making your habit stick. Once you know which one, you can make adjustments as necessary.

  • Do you have enough time?
  • Do you have enough money?
  • Are you physically capable?
  • Do you have enough creative or mental energy?
  • Does it fit into your routine?

Are you just forgetting to do your habit?

Rehearsing your habit

Practice your prompt, habit, and celebration 10 times in a row. Make sure that your celebration is creating an authentic internal feeling of success.This will drastically increase how quickly you pick up the habit.

How do Tiny Habits grow?

Success Growth

When you apply the Tiny Habits method consistently, your habits will scale naturally.

Success leads to success. But here’s something that may surprise you. The size of the success doesn’t seem to matter very much. When you feel successful at something, even if it’s tiny, your confidence grows quickly, and your motivation increases to do that habit again and perform related behaviors.

I call this success momentum. Surprisingly enough, this gets created by the frequency of your successes, not by the size. So with Tiny Habits you are shooting for a bunch of tiny successes done quickly. Not a big one that takes a long time.

When people feel successful, even with small things, their overall level of motivation goes up dramatically, and with higher levels of motivation, people can do harder behaviors. This is how tiny successes can change the game for you at work, at home, and even inside your own head.

What do I need to remember?

Tips

  • Don’t pressure yourself to do more than the tiniest version of your habit.
  • Don't restrict yourself from going bigger if you want to do more.
  • If you do too much, make sure you celebrate extra hard.
  • Use emotional flags to help you find your edge. Frustration, pain, avoidance, and boredom, are all signs that changes need to be made to the habit.
  • Don't raise the bar on your habit prematurely.
  • Positive emotions and consistency are the most important factors for making a habit stick and grow.

The big takeaway: Start where you want to on your path to change. Allow yourself to feel successful. Then trust the process.

Untangling Bad Habits

Use everything you learned about making habits easier in reverse.

When it comes to stopping a bad habit, a common mistake is trying to motivate yourself toward an abstraction, such as “stop stressing out at work” or “stop eating junk food.” Those both sound specific, but they are not. They are abstract labels for a tangle of habits, what I call the General Habit. If you focus only on the General Habit, you probably won’t make much progress, just like if you focus on the entire knot at once, you can’t untangle it. You need to focus on specific tangles in order to make progress. That means finding specific habits to focus on.

Use the Swarm of Behaviors cloud from earlier to help clarify the particular behaviors that are part of your bad habit:

  1. Write the general bad behavior in the cloud
  2. Write specific behaviors around it

Next, choose the easiest habits to get rid of first. Then, use the momentum and confidence you build to tackle harder things as you feel ready for them.

You can use the Ability Chain to figure out why each unwanted behavior is easy to do, and then strengthen the weakest link to make it even harder to do. You can also Scale Down your bad habit by stopping for only a week, or doing it for less time each day.
