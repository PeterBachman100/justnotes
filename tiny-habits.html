Tiny Habits: The Small Changes that Change Everything

- BJ Fogg

Creating good habits (and "untangling" unwanted ones) can be both easy and fun.

"Behavior Design" is based on Dr. BJ Fogg's 20 years of research and personal coaching experience as a Behavior Scientist at Stanford University.

Fogg Behavior Model

Behavior = Motivation + Ability + Prompt

A Behavior will happen if both Motivation and Ability are high enough when the Prompt occurs.

Clarify The Aspiration

What do you actually want?

Our actual goal can often be hidden behind what we think will get us there. For example, some people want to meditate 15 minutes a day, but the actual goal is reduced stress, or improved focus. They fail time and time again to achieve their goal, because meditating is not the right fit for them.

There are plenty of ways to reduce stress and improve focus that aren't meditating. But if you don't realize your True Aspiration, you will never see the easier path.

We should be dreamy about our aspirations, but not about the behaviors that get us there.

4 / 7

Start Tiny

Consistency is the second most important part of behavior design.

When you design a new habit, you are designing for consistency, for which simplicity is key. By going tiny, you create consistency, which makes sure the habit sticks.

There are many ways to make a behavior easier to do:

  • Increase skills
  • Get tools and resources
  • Make the behavior tiny by Scaling Back or using a Starter Step.

Scaling Back: Shrinking your desired behavior. (Walk 10 steps instead of 3 miles)

Starter Step: One small move toward your desired behavior. (Put on your shoes instead of walking 3 miles)

In any case, the tiniest version counts as success, and it is critical that you see it, and celebrate it, as such.

Find a Good Prompt

Prompts can be based off personal, internal cues (you feel hungry), external cues (a sticky note or alarm), or based off actions (your feet hit the floor after waking up). Action-based prompts are by far the most reliable. These are called Anchor Moments, or Anchors for short.

Using Anchors as Cues for Habits

As a personal resource, make a list of all moments that happen reliably throughout your day, using these sections as a guide. Here are some of mine:

Morning

Wake up, feet hit the floor, go pee, open bedroom door, turn on lights, enter kitchen, open fridge, finish eating, brush teeth, close front door, say goodbye to Tiffany, pass the garbage, re-enter the complex, enter the front door, unclip Mia, close the closet door.

Work

Open Laptop, startup programs, open email, open OneNote, start a meeting, end a meeting, pomodoro timer starts, pomodoro timer ends, 4pm.

Lunch

11:45, open fridge, close fridge, first bite, last bite, open/close front door.

When using Anchors, try to find something that defines a precise, final moment of the action, called the Trailing Edge. For example, the Trailing Edge of brushing my teeth is putting my toothbrush back in the holder. Using the Trailing Edge of an Anchor is much more reliable than using the general moment.

For the best results, the Anchor (Prompt) and Behavior should have the same Physical Location, Theme/Purpose, and Frequency.

  • Physical location: the anchor and behavior need to happen in the same location
  • Frequency: If you want to do something 4 times a day, match it with an anchor that happens 4 times a day
  • Theme/purpose: for the best results, match productivity behaviors with productivity anchors, relaxing behaviors with relaxing anchors, etc.
The habit recipe "After I sit down for work, I will change my car oil" will almost certainly fail because the location, frequency, and theme don't match.

You can use irritants and in-between times in your life as prompts for behaviors, such as hearing your coworker complain, or waiting in line. These are respectively called Pearl and Meanwhile habits.

6 / 7

Celebrate Successes

This is the most important part of creating habits!

Emotions Create Habits

I change best by feeling good, not by feeling bad.

When you celebrate effectively , you tap into the reward circuitry of your brain. By feeling good at the right moment , you cause your brain to recognize and encode the sequence of behaviors you just performed. In other words , you can hack your brain to create a habit by celebrating and self - reinforcing .

Habits can form in just a few days if there is a strong positive emotion connected.

Conversely, if a behavior is painful, awkward, or unpleasant, your brain will rewire to start avoiding that habit. To keep a habit alive, celebrate extra when it is uncomfortable.

Use celebration anytime you want to reinforce a behavior in yourself. Celebration will one day be ranked alongside mindfulness and gratitude as daily practices that contribute most to our happiness and well-being.

Celebrations need to feel authentic and create a feeling of "Shine" to be effective. Here are some celebrations that work for me:

  • Consider how your new habit helps you achieve your life's purpose.
  • Imagine getting the news that you got a new job.
  • Pause, breathe deeply, and appreciate your success.
  • Inhale and think of energy entering you.
  • Pound your chest.
  • Fist pump.
  • Thank God.
  • Play the song "Get Up" by T-pain.
  • Flex.
  • Say or think: "Good Job", "That's it", "YES", "F*** yeah"
  • Visualize God saying "Well done good and faithful servant"

Troubleshoot, Iterate, and Expand

You can use the Ability Chain to analyze a habit and make it easier or harder, depending on if you want to make or break a habit

Start with the question: What is making the behavior hard to do?

  • Do you have enough time?
  • Do you have enough money?
  • Are you physically capable?
  • Do you have enough creative or mental energy?
  • Does it fit into your routine?

Rehearse your habit!

Practice your prompt, habit, and celebration 10 times in a row. This will drastically increase how quickly you pick up the habit.

How To Start

Start where you want. Let your habits grow naturally. Celebrate the whole way!

Big Change Comes From Small, Incremental Progress

  • Stop judging yourself
  • Don't raise the bar on your habit prematurely. Positive emotions and consistency are the most important factors for making a habit stick and grow.
  • Break down your aspirations into specific behaviors
  • Celebrate every success
  • Embrace mistakes as discoveries and use them to move forward
  • You ARE doing something worth celebrating.
